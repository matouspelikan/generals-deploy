## Key Concepts

**Information retrieval (IR):** the task of finding material (usually documents) that satisfies an information need from a large collection.

**Information need:** the underlying topic or question a user wants answered, which may be vague and is distinct from the query they actually type.

**Query:** the explicit expression of an information need submitted to an IR system, typically a short string of keywords or a Boolean expression.

**Document relevance:** a document is relevant to a query if it satisfies the underlying information need; relevance is often graded and subjective rather than binary.

**Boolean model:** an IR model where documents and queries are represented as sets of terms and retrieval is based on exact Boolean logic (AND, OR, NOT). A document is either retrieved or not.

**Boolean query:** a query expressed using Boolean operators; e.g. `cat AND dog NOT fish`.

**Word / Term / Token:** a *word* is a natural-language unit; a *token* is an instance of a word in running text (including duplicates); a *term* is a normalized, indexed unit (after case folding, stemming, etc.) used as the dictionary entry.

**Text normalization:** transforming raw text into a canonical form before indexing — includes lowercasing, punctuation removal, tokenization, stemming, and lemmatization.

**Stemming:** a rule-based process that strips word endings to reduce variants to a common root, which may not be a real word (e.g. `running` → `run`, `university` → `univers`). Fast but imprecise.

**Lemmatization:** reducing a word to its proper dictionary base form (lemma) using vocabulary and morphological analysis (e.g. `better` → `good`, `was` → `be`). More accurate than stemming but slower.

**Stopwords:** very frequent function words (e.g. *the*, *is*, *of*) that carry little discriminative information and are often removed to reduce index size.

**Stopword removal:** the process of filtering out stopwords before indexing or query processing; improves efficiency but can hurt phrase query handling.

**Inverted index:** the core data structure of IR — a mapping from each term to a list of documents (and optionally positions) in which it appears.

**Dictionary:** the sorted list of all distinct terms in the inverted index; one entry per term, storing metadata such as document frequency and a pointer to the postings list.

**Postings:** the list associated with a dictionary term, recording which documents contain the term (and optionally where). Each entry is called a posting.

**Postings intersection:** the merge algorithm that computes the AND of two sorted postings lists in linear time by scanning both lists simultaneously.

**Biword index:** an index over consecutive word pairs (bigrams), such as `"information retrieval"`, used to support two-word phrase queries without positional data.

**Positional index:** an inverted index that stores the position(s) of each token within each document, enabling exact phrase and proximity queries.

**Phrasal search:** retrieval of documents containing an exact sequence of words (e.g. `"New York Times"`), implemented using a positional index.

**Proximity search:** retrieval of documents where two or more terms appear within a specified distance of each other (e.g. within 5 words), also implemented using a positional index.

## Exercise 1.1

> Draw the postings for terms rise and home that would be built for the following document collection:
>
> | Doc | text |
> | --- | --- |
> | Doc1 | new home sales top forecasts |
> | Doc2 | home sales rise in july |
> | Doc3 | increase in home sales in july |
> | Doc4 | july new home sales rise |

Using lowercased terms and document-only postings:

| term | postings |
| --- | --- |
| rise | Doc2, Doc4 |
| home | Doc1, Doc2, Doc3, Doc4 |

If the index is positional, with token positions counted from 1:

| term | positional postings |
| --- | --- |
| rise | Doc2: 3; Doc4: 5 |
| home | Doc1: 2; Doc2: 1; Doc3: 3; Doc4: 3 |

The document-only postings record that a term occurs in a document. The positional postings additionally record where the term occurs, which is needed for phrase and proximity queries.

## Exercise 1.2

> Query optimization is the process of selecting how to organize the work of answering a query so that the least total amount of work needs to be done by the system. Recommend a query processing order for the following two queries:
>
> - `trees AND skies AND kaleidoscope`
> - `(tangerine OR trees) AND (marmalade OR skies) AND (kaleidoscope OR eyes)`
>
> and the postings list sizes:
>
> | Term | Postings Size |
> | --- | ---: |
> | eyes | 213,312 |
> | kaleidoscope | 87,009 |
> | marmalade | 107,913 |
> | skies | 271,658 |
> | tangerine | 46,653 |
> | trees | 316,812 |

For `trees AND skies AND kaleidoscope`, process the conjuncts in increasing postings-list size:

1. `kaleidoscope` - 87,009
2. `skies` - 271,658
3. `trees` - 316,812

So a good order is:

```text
(kaleidoscope AND skies) AND trees
```

For `(tangerine OR trees) AND (marmalade OR skies) AND (kaleidoscope OR eyes)`, first estimate the size of each disjunctive clause by adding the postings sizes. This gives an upper bound because the two lists may overlap:

| clause | estimated size |
| --- | ---: |
| `kaleidoscope OR eyes` | 87,009 + 213,312 = 300,321 |
| `tangerine OR trees` | 46,653 + 316,812 = 363,465 |
| `marmalade OR skies` | 107,913 + 271,658 = 379,571 |

Then process the resulting conjuncts from smallest to largest:

```text
(kaleidoscope OR eyes)
AND (tangerine OR trees)
AND (marmalade OR skies)
```

Within each `OR`, the two postings lists are merged to form the union. The main saving comes from doing the smallest conjunctive intermediate result first.

## Exercise 1.3

> For a conjunctive query, is processing postings lists in order of size guaranteed to be optimal? Explain why it is, or give an example where it isn't.

No. Processing postings lists in increasing size is a very good heuristic, but it is not guaranteed to be optimal because the sizes of intermediate intersections depend on how the postings lists overlap.

Example:

```text
A = {1, 2, ..., 99}        size 99
B = {1, 2, ..., 100}       size 100
C = {101, 102, ..., 200}   size 100
```

For the query `A AND B AND C`, increasing-size order starts with `A AND B`, producing `{1, 2, ..., 99}`, and then intersects that with `C`, producing the empty set. But `B AND C` is already empty, so starting with `B AND C` can terminate the query earlier. The smaller-first order did not exploit the fact that the two larger postings lists were disjoint.

Thus postings-list size alone is not enough information for a guaranteed optimal order; overlap and early termination also matter.

## Exercise 1.4

> Write out a postings merge algorithm for the query:
>
> `x OR y`

For `x OR y`, return the union of the two sorted postings lists. The merge is similar to intersection, but we output every document that occurs in either list and avoid duplicates.

```text
Union(p1, p2)
    answer <- <>

    while p1 != nil and p2 != nil
        if docID(p1) = docID(p2)
            Add(answer, docID(p1))
            p1 <- next(p1)
            p2 <- next(p2)
        else if docID(p1) < docID(p2)
            Add(answer, docID(p1))
            p1 <- next(p1)
        else
            Add(answer, docID(p2))
            p2 <- next(p2)

    while p1 != nil
        Add(answer, docID(p1))
        p1 <- next(p1)

    while p2 != nil
        Add(answer, docID(p2))
        p2 <- next(p2)

    return answer
```

This is linear in the lengths of the two postings lists.

## Exercise 1.5

> How should the following Boolean query be handled?
>
> `x AND NOT y`
>
> Why is naive evaluation of this query normally very expensive? Write out a postings merge algorithm that evaluates this query efficiently.

The query `x AND NOT y` should be handled as a set difference:

```text
postings(x) - postings(y)
```

Naive evaluation is expensive because `NOT y` means all documents in the collection that do not contain `y`. Materializing this complement can be enormous: if the collection has `N` documents and `y` occurs in only a small number of them, then `NOT y` is almost the whole collection. Intersecting `x` with that huge list wastes work.

Instead, scan `x` and `y` together and output only documents from `x` that are not in `y`:

```text
Difference(p1, p2)    // p1 = postings(x), p2 = postings(y)
    answer <- <>

    while p1 != nil and p2 != nil
        if docID(p1) = docID(p2)
            p1 <- next(p1)
            p2 <- next(p2)
        else if docID(p1) < docID(p2)
            Add(answer, docID(p1))
            p1 <- next(p1)
        else
            p2 <- next(p2)

    while p1 != nil
        Add(answer, docID(p1))
        p1 <- next(p1)

    return answer
```

This avoids constructing the complement of `y`. The output can never be larger than `postings(x)`.

## Exercise 1.6

> Are the following statements true or false?
>
> 1. In a Boolean retrieval system, stemming never lowers precision.
> 2. In a Boolean retrieval system, stemming never lowers recall.
> 3. Stemming increases the size of the vocabulary.
> 4. Stemming should be invoked at indexing time but not while processing a query.

| statement | true/false | explanation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| In a Boolean retrieval system, stemming never lowers precision. | False | Stemming can conflate distinct meanings, causing false positives. For example, conflating `universe` and `university` would retrieve documents about one when the user wanted the other. |
| In a Boolean retrieval system, stemming never lowers recall. | False as an absolute statement | For simple positive term queries, consistent stemming normally preserves or increases recall because more variants match. But Boolean queries may include operators such as `NOT`; after stemming, a negative term can exclude more documents than intended. So "never" is too strong. |
| Stemming increases the size of the vocabulary. | False | Stemming maps multiple surface forms to one stem, so it normally reduces the vocabulary size. |
| Stemming should be invoked at indexing time but not while processing a query. | False | Stemming must be applied consistently at indexing time and query time. Otherwise query terms may not match the stemmed terms stored in the dictionary. |

## Exercise 1.7

> The following pairs of words are stemmed to the same form by the Porter stemmer. Which pairs would you argue shouldn't be conflated. Give your reasoning.
>
> - abandon/abandonment
> - absorbency/absorbent
> - marketing/markets
> - university/universe
> - volume/volumes

Pairs I would not conflate:

| pair | should they be conflated? | reasoning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `abandon` / `abandonment` | Usually acceptable | These are closely related verb/noun forms. A user interested in one will often accept documents containing the other. |
| `absorbency` / `absorbent` | Debatable, often acceptable | `absorbency` is the property and `absorbent` is the adjective or noun for something having that property. They are not identical, but in many IR settings the semantic relation is close enough. |
| `marketing` / `markets` | Often should not be conflated | `marketing` means the activity or field, while `markets` can mean places, institutions, or plural instances of `market`. Some queries about financial markets should not be expanded to marketing. |
| `university` / `universe` | Should not be conflated | These have unrelated meanings despite similar spelling. Conflating them would significantly hurt precision. |
| `volume` / `volumes` | Should be conflated | This is a regular singular/plural inflection and is normally exactly the kind of variation stemming should handle. |

The most clearly wrong conflation is `university` / `universe`. The pair `marketing` / `markets` is also risky because the meanings can diverge substantially depending on the query.
