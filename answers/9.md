## Key Concepts

**Language models for IR:** model each document as a probability distribution over words. A document is relevant if it likely generated the query. Ranking is by P(query | document model).

**Parameter estimation:** estimating the probability of each term in a document's language model, typically using Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE): P(t | d) = count(t, d) / |d|. MLE assigns zero probability to unseen terms, which is a problem.

**Smoothing:** addresses the zero-probability problem by mixing the document model with a background (collection) model, ensuring every term gets non-zero probability. Essential for good retrieval performance.

**Jelinek-Mercer smoothing:** linear interpolation of the document model and collection model: P(t | d) = λ·P(t | M_d) + (1−λ)·P(t | M_c). The parameter λ controls how much to trust the document vs. the collection.

**Dirichlet smoothing:** a Bayesian smoothing method that adds a prior based on the collection model. The effective mixture weight depends on document length: short documents are smoothed more toward the collection. Generally preferred over Jelinek-Mercer in practice.

**Text classification:** assigning a document to one or more predefined categories automatically. Training uses labeled examples; the learned model predicts labels for new documents.

**Naive Bayes classification:** a probabilistic classifier based on Bayes' theorem with the naive assumption that features (words) are conditionally independent given the class. Despite the unrealistic independence assumption, it works well in practice.

**Naive Bayes classification rule:** assign the class c* = argmax_c P(c) · ∏_t P(t | c). The prior P(c) favors frequent classes; the likelihood ∏_t P(t | c) captures how well the word sequence fits the class.

**Naive Bayes parameter estimation:** P(c) = (number of training docs in class c) / (total docs); P(t | c) = (count of term t in class c documents) / (total term count in class c documents).

**Naive Bayes smoothing:** add-one (Laplace) smoothing prevents zero probabilities for terms not seen in a class during training: P(t | c) = (count(t, c) + 1) / (total tokens in c + |V|).

**Evaluation of classification:** uses Precision, Recall, and F-measure computed per class. Micro-averaging aggregates TP/FP/FN counts over all classes before computing the metric (favors large classes). Macro-averaging computes the metric per class and then averages (treats all classes equally).

**One-class (single-label) vs. multi-class (multi-label) classification:** single-label assigns exactly one class per document (mutually exclusive classes); multi-label allows multiple classes per document simultaneously (each class is an independent binary decision).

**Micro vs. macro averaging:** micro-averaging computes metrics globally across all class decisions; macro-averaging computes per-class metrics and then takes the mean, giving equal weight to each class regardless of size.

## Exercise 9.1

> Including STOP probabilities in the calculation, what will the sum of the probability estimates of all strings in the language of length 1 be? Assume that you generate a word and then decide whether to stop or not (i.e., the null string is not part of the language).

For a string of length 1, the model first generates one word and then decides whether to stop.

Let the vocabulary words be `w`, excluding the special `STOP` symbol. The probability of generating exactly the one-word string `w` is:

```text
P(w) * P(STOP)
```

So the sum of probabilities of all strings of length 1 is:

```text
sum_w P(w) * P(STOP)
= P(STOP) * sum_w P(w)
```

Since the probabilities over normal words plus `STOP` sum to 1:

```text
sum_w P(w) = 1 - P(STOP)
```

Therefore:

```text
sum of probabilities of all length-1 strings
= P(STOP) * (1 - P(STOP))
```

This is less than 1 because the language model also assigns probability to strings of other lengths.

## Exercise 9.2

> How might a language model be used in a spelling correction system? In particular, consider the case of context-sensitive spelling correction, and correcting incorrect usages of words, such as *their* in *Are you their?*

A language model can be used in spelling correction by combining two probabilities:

```text
P(intended correction | observed text)
```

This is often decomposed into:

```text
P(observed error | intended correction) * P(intended correction in context)
```

The first part is the error model, often based on edit distance or weighted edit distance. The second part is the language model, which says how plausible a candidate correction is in the surrounding context.

For context-sensitive spelling correction, the language model is crucial because the incorrect word may itself be a valid word. In:

```text
Are you their?
```

isolated-term correction may not flag `their`, since it is spelled correctly. A language model can compare candidates such as:

```text
Are you their
Are you there
Are you they're
```

The sequence `Are you there` has much higher probability under a good language model, so `there` is the preferred correction.

## Exercise 9.3

> Consider making a language model from the following training text:
>
> *the martian has landed on the latin pop sensation ricky martin*
>
> 1. Under a MLE-estimated unigram probability model, what are P(the) and P(martian)?
> 2. Under a MLE-estimated bigram model, what are P(sensation|pop) and P(pop|the)?

Training text:

```text
the martian has landed on the latin pop sensation ricky martin
```

There are 11 tokens. The unigram counts include:

```text
count(the) = 2
count(martian) = 1
```

So under MLE unigram estimation:

```text
P(the) = 2/11
P(martian) = 1/11
```

For the bigram model:

```text
P(sensation | pop) = count(pop sensation) / count(pop)
                   = 1 / 1
                   = 1
```

The word `the` occurs twice, followed by `martian` once and `latin` once. It is never followed by `pop`:

```text
P(pop | the) = count(the pop) / count(the)
             = 0 / 2
             = 0
```

## Exercise 9.4

> Suppose we have a collection that consists of the 4 documents given in the below table.
>
> | DocID | Document text |
> | --- | --- |
> | Doc1 | click go the shears boys click click click |
> | Doc2 | click click |
> | Doc3 | metal here |
> | Doc4 | metal shears click here |
>
> Build a query likelihood language model for this document collection. Assume a mixture model between the documents and the collection, with both weighted at 0.5. Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) is used to estimate both as unigram models. Work out the model probabilities of the queries click, shears, and hence click shears for each document, and use those probabilities to rank the documents returned by each query. What is the final ranking of the documents for the query click shears?

The collection is:

| document | text | length |
| --- | --- | ---: |
| Doc1 | `click go the shears boys click click click` | 8 |
| Doc2 | `click click` | 2 |
| Doc3 | `metal here` | 2 |
| Doc4 | `metal shears click here` | 4 |

The collection has 16 tokens total. Collection frequencies:

```text
cf(click) = 7
cf(shears) = 2
```

So:

```text
P(click | M_c) = 7/16 = 0.4375
P(shears | M_c) = 2/16 = 0.125
```

The mixture model weights the document model and collection model equally:

```text
P(t | d) = 0.5 * P(t | M_d) + 0.5 * P(t | M_c)
```

The resulting probabilities are:

| query | Doc1 | Doc2 | Doc3 | Doc4 |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| `click` | 0.46875 | 0.71875 | 0.21875 | 0.34375 |
| `shears` | 0.12500 | 0.06250 | 0.06250 | 0.18750 |
| `click shears` | 0.05859 | 0.04492 | 0.01367 | 0.06445 |

For example, for `click` in Doc1:

```text
P(click | Doc1) = 0.5 * (4/8) + 0.5 * (7/16)
                = 0.25 + 0.21875
                = 0.46875
```

For a multi-term query, use the unigram independence assumption and multiply the term probabilities:

```text
P(click shears | d) = P(click | d) * P(shears | d)
```

The final ranking for `click shears` is:

```text
Doc4 > Doc1 > Doc2 > Doc3
```

## Exercise 9.5

> The rationale for the positional independence assumption is that there is no useful information in the fact that a term occurs in position k of a document. Find exceptions. Consider formulaic documents with a fixed document structure.

The positional independence assumption says that a term's probability does not depend on its position in the document. This is often a reasonable bag-of-words approximation, but formulaic documents can violate it badly.

Examples:

- In news articles, words in the headline or first paragraph are often more important than words near the end.
- In scientific papers, a term in the title, abstract, or conclusion may be more informative than the same term in the bibliography.
- In email, a word in the subject line often matters more than a word in a quoted previous message.
- In resumes, a company name in the work-experience section means something different from the same name in a references section.
- In legal or medical forms, fixed fields such as `diagnosis`, `allergies`, `defendant`, or `date` give positional or structural meaning to terms.

So position and document structure can carry useful information, especially when documents have predictable fields.
